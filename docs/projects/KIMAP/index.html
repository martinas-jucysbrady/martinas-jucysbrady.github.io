0. Executive Summary

This advisory report, prepared for the European Commission, contains strategic recommendations to avoid conflicts and improve the success rate of EU-funded multinational projects. Drawing on insights from Jackson & Rasheed’s case study on the European Palestinian Project Office (EPPO), it identifies key governance challenges and offers practical solutions to improve project efficiency and collaboration in diverse, cross-border environments.

The governance of multinational projects is fraught with significant challenges, including financial mismanagement, political interference, cultural misalignment and weak conflict resolution mechanisms. To address these issues, this report proposes a governance framework based on four key pillars:

Cross-Cultural Integration

Conducting cultural audits, implementing inclusive policies and drawing from Singapore’s ethnic integration policy will help bridge cultural divides, improve communication and foster greater collaboration between project teams.

Conflict Resolution and Reconciliation

Establishing structured conflict resolution mechanisms modelled on El Salvador’s multi-layered security approach to security and South Africa’s Truth and Reconciliation Commission will create a proactive system to resolve disputes before they escalate.

Optimized Resource Allocation

The introduction of performance-based funding, inspired by South Korea’s post-war development strategy, will ensure an efficient allocation of financial and human resources. Transparent salary structures and targeted investments will improve the accountability and sustainability of projects.

By adopting these strategies, the European Commission can minimize governance-related risks, improve cooperation and strengthen the long-term impact of EU-funded projects. These recommendations aim to foster more resilient, efficient and sustainable international partnerships reinforcing the EU’s leadership in global development.

1. Introduction

This consultancy report has been prepared for a panel of project assessors at the European Commission, with the aim of providing actionable recommendations to help avoid conflicts and performance failures in future EU funded multinational projects. Drawing on the insights from the Jackson & Rasheed case study of the European-Palestinian Project Office (EPPO), this report outlines key strategies to address common challenges that can arise during cross-border collaborations. The EPPO case serves as a practical example of the complexities involved in large-scale multinational projects, where differing interests, cultural dynamics, and resource allocation issues can lead to significant delays and inefficiencies.

In response to these challenges, this report proposes a set of objectives for the European Commission to adopt in its approach to managing future projects. These objectives focus on integrating cross-cultural intelligence, establishing robust conflict-prevention frameworks, optimising resource allocation, and promoting inclusive decision-making processes. By implementing these recommendations, the European Commission can not only mitigate risks but also enhance collaboration and foster a more cohesive working environment across diverse project teams. Ultimately, these strategies aim to reinforce the EU’s broader goals of unity, innovation, and sustainable growth in an increasingly complex and interconnected global landscape.

2. Background of the Report

This consultancy report has been commissioned to support the European Commission’s Unit for External Governance, Transparency, and Compliance (EGTC)’s ongoing efforts to strengthen governance oversight and conflict avoidance in EU-funded projects. The report will assess existing practices, identify potential gaps or weaknesses, and provide strategic recommendations for improving the unit’s operational framework. The report will also explore lessons learned from past governance failures, such as the EPPO case, and offer insights into how these lessons can inform future project management practices. By drawing on global best practices, including examples from countries such as Singapore and El Salvador, the report will provide recommendations for enhancing the EGTC’s ability to prevent conflicts, improve governance structures, and ensure the successful delivery of EU-funded projects in politically fragile regions (Dent 2001; Wolf 2012). The consultancy report will play a crucial role in helping the EGTC meet its mission to promote transparency, accountability, and sustainability in international development.

The framework provided will draw on practices which emphasise proactive conflict prevention instead of reactive crisis management. For instance, Venezuela’s experience with international cooperation underscores the need to navigate political instability and economic challenges in cross-border initiatives (Duddy 2015). By fostering diplomatic dialogue and adaptive management strategies, the European Commission can draw lessons from Venezuela’s challenges to strengthen resilience and ensure the success of EU-funded multinational projects. These examples serve as a guide for the European Commission to refine its project management methodologies, ensuring they are resilient, equitable, and responsive to the diverse needs of EU stakeholders.

2.2 Global Challenges and Lessons from Past Governance Failures

The global context in which the EGTC operates has become increasingly complex in recent years. International development projects are often implemented in politically unstable and conflict-prone regions, where governance structures are weak, and security risks are high. Instances of governance failures in EU-funded projects, such as financial mismanagement, political interference, and corruption, have underscored the vulnerability of these initiatives. High-profile cases, including the mismanagement of EU-funded aid highlight the need for more robust oversight and accountability measures. Furthermore, lessons can be drawn from the experiences of countries like Venezuela, where political instability and economic crises have posed significant challenges for international cooperation (Duddy 2015). The EGTC must navigate these complex geopolitical landscapes and learn from past mistakes to strengthen its governance frameworks and improve the effectiveness of future EU-funded projects.

4. Objectives for mitigating conflict and enhancing multinational collaboration

4.1 Cross cultural integration

To prevent cultural misalignment and enhance the success of EU funded projects, several strategies can be adopted. First, the European Commission should conduct pre-project cultural audits to identify differences in cultural values, including power distance, individualism, and uncertainty avoidance, among team members. Using a combination of Hofstede’s cultural dimensions theory, and similar frameworks such as Trompenaars’ Seven Dimensions of Culture, the identification of cultural differences is made much easier. These audits would allow the European Commission to tailor communication and negotiation strategies to suit the cultural context of each project’s stakeholders. Additionally, implementing mandatory cultural training programs for project managers and stakeholders would help prepare them to navigate differences in communication styles, conflict resolution, and decision-making processes (Hofstede 1984). For example, a cultural clash arises when a French manager’s emphasis on hierarchical professionalism and universal rules conflicts with a Swiss employee’s preference for local traditions and a more egalitarian workplace structure. The manager’s demand to remove personal items from an office setting would highlight France’s high-context reliance on implicit workplace norms, while the Swiss employee’s resistance would reflect a blend of neutrality and emotional expression. This tension is a direct result of the contrasting cultural dimensions of power distance, particularism vs. universalism, and neutral vs. emotional communication (Jackson 2013).

A global example of successfully managing cultural diversity comes from Singapore’s Ethnic Integration Policy (EIP), introduced in 1989. The EIP mandates ethnic quotas in public housing to ensure a balanced representation of Chinese, Malay, and Indian communities, preventing the formation of ethnic enclaves and fostering intercultural dialogue (Lim, Leong, and Suliman 2019). Although this example covers the area of diversification in Singapore’s neighbourhoods, the EU commission must consider the success of such policies in other areas that need improvement. The success of the EIP hinges on three principles: anticipatory governance (which addresses potential cultural divides before they solidify), structural incentives (such as policy tools to encourage integration) and community agency, empowering local leaders to mediate disputes (Sim, Yu, and Han 2003). Singapore’s model demonstrates the value of proactively embedding cultural intelligence into project design, ensuring that cultural differences are addressed from the outset.

Cultural misalignment in multinational projects is not an inevitable outcome, but rather, it is a manageable risk. By learning from successful examples like Singapore’s anticipatory governance, the European Commission can turn cultural diversity from a potential obstacle into a strategic asset. By integrating cultural intelligence into project design (by conducting cultural audits, offering targeted training, creating mediation teams, and establishing advisory boards), the European Commission can foster better communication, increase trust among stakeholders, and enhance project outcomes. With these proactive measures, the EU can ensure that cultural differences are not a barrier, but a source of strength, leading to more successful and sustainable collaborations between team members.

The EPPO case study demonstrates how a lack of structured cultural integration can lead to workplace division and inefficiencies. At EPPO, some staff often spoke Spanish among themselves, unintentionally excluding Palestinian employees from key discussions. This created communication barriers, social fragmentation, and a lack of trust, ultimately affecting collaboration. A structured approach, like Singapore’s Ethnic Integration Policy (EIP), could have mitigated this issue. Singapore’s EIP proactively prevents ethnic enclaves by ensuring diverse residential communities, fostering integration through policy-driven inclusivity rather than leaving it to chance. Applying a similar model, the European Commission should conduct pre-project cultural audits and implement mandatory language inclusivity policies to ensure all team members, regardless of nationality, feel equally involved. This would have prevented the communication breakdowns at EPPO and promoted a more cohesive working environment from the start.

4.2 Conflict resolution frameworks

Effective conflict resolution is critical for the success of multinational projects, particularly those funded by the European Commission. Conflict is an inherent risk in cross-border collaborations, where differing interests, values, and communication styles can lead to misunderstandings, delays, or even project failure. To ensure smoother project execution, it is essential to create systems that address conflicts before they escalate. The goal of this objective is to establish proactive conflict-resolution frameworks that not only resolve disputes but also mitigate the risk of conflict arising in the first place. Drawing from global examples of successful conflict resolution, such as El Salvador’s extreme reduction in gang violence, the European Commission can take note of frameworks used in order to create a more resilient and cooperative environment for multinational projects.

El Salvador’s success in reducing gang violence (from one of the world’s highest homicide rates in 2015 to a 70% drop by 2023) offers a model for blending security and social reforms. Recognising that violence is not only a security issue but also a social and economic one, El Salvador adopted a hybrid approach, combining law enforcement with targeted social policies aimed at addressing the root causes of violence (Rosen, Cutrona, and Lindquist 2023). This comprehensive strategy focused on addressing issues such as poverty, education, and lack of opportunities, alongside traditional security measures. The initiative also emphasized community participation, where local leaders and citizens played an active role in building trust and resolving conflicts at the grassroots level (Menjívar 2024; El Salvador News 2024a, 2024b). El Salvador’s experience underscores the importance of addressing both the symptoms and root causes of conflict, ensuring that efforts to resolve disputes are not only reactive but also proactive.

Although this example encompassing El Salvador’s conflict is one with a dark, violent and troubling history, the European Commission can learn from El Salvador’s example by adopting a similar multilayered approach to conflict resolution in more professional environments. A key recommendation is the development of steps in conflict resolution, which can address conflicts at various stages of escalation. These steps should involve three levels: mediation, arbitration, and, if necessary, escalation. The first step of conflict resolution should be mediation, where neutral third parties help the conflicting parties communicate and seek a mutually acceptable solution. If mediation fails to resolve the issue, the next step should be arbitration, where an independent authority makes a binding decision to resolve the conflict. Only in cases where these methods fail should escalation to higher authorities or legal action be considered. By implementing these steps, the European Commission can ensure that conflicts are dealt with at the earliest possible stage, preventing them from escalating into more serious issues that could threaten project success. The European Commission must treat illegal actions strongly to ensure that it is discouraged and not ignored (similar to El Salvador’s complete security crackdown), and they must invest in early conflict identification and mediation to ensure that small issues don’t become large ones (similar to El Salvador’s investment in communities within the country). This second point also draws back to the first objective of cross cultural integration as diverse cultures have a higher likelihood to be a source of conflict within a project.

The EPPO case study illustrates how the absence of clear conflict resolution mechanisms can escalate tensions and create a toxic workplace culture. At EPPO, Miguel’s aggressive leadership style and lack of respect for boundaries, such as breaking into Eman’s and Ahmed’s desks, led to feelings of powerlessness and frustration among employees. Since there was no formal conflict resolution structure, issues were either ignored or left to fester, ultimately undermining the organization’s mission. A tiered conflict-resolution approach, like El Salvador’s hybrid model of security enforcement and social reform, could have helped address these tensions early on. In El Salvador, crime prevention efforts combined strong enforcement with proactive mediation and social programs, reducing violence by tackling both the symptoms and root causes of conflict. Similarly, the European Commission should establish structured conflict-resolution protocols within multinational projects, combining mediation, arbitration, and structured escalation paths. Had EPPO implemented such a system, disputes could have been de-escalated before they disrupted team cohesion and operational effectiveness.

4.3 Conflict reconciliation

Conflict reconciliation is a critical element in ensuring the success of multinational projects. South Africa’s Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) provides an instructive model for how a restorative justice approach can create space for healing and foster collaboration in post-conflict societies (Van der Merwe and Chapman 2008). For EU-funded multinational projects, particularly those involving diverse stakeholders with complex backgrounds, integrating elements of the TRC model can help address tensions while strengthening the foundations for future cooperation.

A central tenet of the TRC’s approach was its focus on truth-telling, where those involved in human rights violations were granted amnesty in exchange for full disclosure of their actions. This allowed South Africa to confront its painful past openly, facilitating a process of healing for victims while encouraging perpetrators to acknowledge the harm they had caused (Van der Merwe and Chapman 2008). For European Commission projects, particularly those with historical or political sensitivities, a similar framework could be used to encourage transparency and dialogue. By establishing mechanisms for stakeholders to openly address past issues, the European Commission can create a cooperative environment in which all parties are able to move forward rather than being mired in unresolved conflict. The lesson from the TRC model should be clear and convey that open communication should be prioritised but must be balanced with actions that address deeper systemic issues to prevent exacerbating existing tensions.

By integrating elements of the TRC model into the conflict reconciliation processes for EU-funded projects, the European Commission can foster an environment where historical tensions are addressed, trust is rebuilt, and stakeholders are empowered to collaborate effectively in the future.

The EPPO case highlights how unaddressed historical tensions and workplace biases can lead to deep divisions within multinational teams. Miguel’s discriminatory remarks toward Palestinian employees, particularly his accusations of corruption against Ahmed, fostered an environment of distrust and widened the gap between European and Palestinian staff. This lack of mutual understanding parallels the challenges South Africa faced post-apartheid, where historical grievances hindered national unity. South Africa’s Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) successfully used structured dialogue and transparency to acknowledge past injustices, rebuilding trust between divided communities. A similar restorative justice model could have benefited EPPO, where open discussions on workplace bias and cross-cultural misunderstandings might have prevented these divisions. For future EU projects, the European Commission should implement structured reconciliation processes, such as facilitated stakeholder dialogues, ensuring that underlying tensions are addressed early, rather than allowing biases and mistrust to undermine long-term collaboration.

4.4 Optimal personnel and resource allocation

Effective resource allocation is a key determinant of the success of EU-funded multinational projects. Ensuring that financial, human, and material resources are used efficiently and strategically can significantly enhance project impact and sustainability. This objective emphasizes not only the proper distribution of financial resources but also the strategic deployment of the right personnel, expertise, and skills to meet long-term project goals. Drawing from global examples, such as South Korea’s targeted post-war investments in education and infrastructure, the European Commission can adopt key strategies to optimize resource allocation for maximum impact, ensuring sustainability and scalability across diverse project environments.

South Korea’s post-war recovery serves as a compelling case in how targeted investments can drive long-term development. After the Korean War (1950–1953), the South Korean government made deliberate investments in key sectors such as education and infrastructure, which aligned with the nation’s cultural values and long-term development goals. This emphasis on education laid the groundwork for South Korea’s rapid industrialization in the 1960s and beyond, enabling the country to emerge as a global economic leader. The success of this model underscores the importance of aligning investments with long-term societal benefits, ensuring both immediate recovery and future economic prosperity (Park, Han, and Lee 2024).

For the European Commission, this example offers several key lessons. One important recommendation is to tie funding to clear, measurable performance metrics. These metrics(stakeholder satisfaction, project milestones, and societal impact) should be established at the outset of each project and regularly assessed to ensure that resources are being used effectively. Projects that consistently fall short of performance benchmarks should be re-evaluated, and resources should be redirected to initiatives that show stronger potential for success. This results based approach helps to avoid resource wastage and ensures that funds are concentrated on projects that deliver tangible outcomes.

Prioritising scalable, sustainable initiatives is essential for achieving a lasting impact. Rather than focusing on short-term, small-scale projects with limited influence, the European Commission should invest in initiatives that offer measurable societal returns and long-term sustainability. This might include projects aimed at creating infrastructure, building local capacity, or promoting economic self-sufficiency. For example, funding initiatives that focus on renewable energy infrastructure or training local leaders can create self-sustaining development that outlasts the initial funding phase, ensuring continued benefits even after the project’s conclusion.

Another crucial strategy for optimising resource allocation involves the “Gordian Knot” principle, which advocates cutting funding to projects with unsolvable conflicts. Some projects face entrenched issues that make success highly unlikely, despite continued investment. Instead of persisting with these projects, the European Commission should consider reallocating resources to more viable alternatives. Inspired by the myth of Alexander the Great, who solved the seemingly impossible puzzle of the Gordian Knot by cutting through it, this principle encourages the redirection of resources from intractable conflicts to initiatives with clearer paths to success. By adopting this principle, the European Commission can ensure that funding is reserved for projects with a higher likelihood of success.

Optimal resource allocation also requires ensuring the right personnel are in place, equipped with the necessary skills and cultural understanding. The European Commission should invest in capacity building programs that not only provide technical training but also foster cultural intelligence. This aligns with the Commission’s broader objectives, such as cross cultural integration, and ensures that project managers and stakeholders are equipped to navigate challenges in diverse environments. By putting the right people in the right roles, the European Commission can maximise the impact of its resource allocation and ensure the success of its projects.

The EPPO case demonstrates how financial mismanagement and resource disparities can cause employee dissatisfaction and operational inefficiencies. Salary discrepancies at EPPO were stark (Miguel was paid 12% more than his allocated budget, while Eman was paid 60% less, despite her essential role in financial management). The lack of transparency in salary structures created resentment and reduced staff morale, impacting overall productivity. A more effective model for resource allocation is South Korea’s post-war economic strategy, which prioritised targeted investments in education and infrastructure to ensure long-term, equitable growth. South Korea’s approach emphasised allocating resources where they had the highest long-term impact, ensuring financial efficiency and sustainable development. Similarly, the European Commission should implement performance-based funding models and regular salary audits to maintain fairness and transparency in multinational projects. Had EPPO followed such a structured resource allocation strategy, financial inequities could have been prevented, fostering a more motivated and engaged workforce.

4.5 Summary

This report outlines a comprehensive approach for the European Commission to enhance the governance of EU-funded multinational projects. By focusing on cross-cultural integration, effective conflict resolution, strategic resource allocation, and fostering inclusive decision-making, the Commission can proactively address potential challenges that often arise in diverse and complex collaborations. Drawing from successful global examples, these strategies provide a clear roadmap for improving communication, trust, and efficiency, ensuring that projects meet their objectives and deliver long-lasting impact.

By implementing these strategies, the European Commission can turn potential obstacles into opportunities for growth and innovation. With a focus on prevention, transparency, and collaboration, these recommendations will not only strengthen the EU’s internal processes but also reinforce its commitment to sustainable development, unity, and global leadership in a rapidly changing world.

5. Observations (Analysis)

Analysis of the EPPO case reveals consistent patterns of mismanagement and governance breakdown. These failures include cultural misalignment, financial disparities, lack of structured conflict resolution, and inefficient allocation of resources. If these issues are not proactively addressed, these issues can lead to underperformance and weakened institutional credibility. This case serves as a microcosm for the potential issues associated with multinational projects. Leadership conflicts, exclusionary cultural practices, and inadequate oversight led to communication breakdowns and inefficiencies. Such incidents are not unique to EPPO, but are symptomatic of many cross-border initiatives where different cultural and political interests clash.

Cultural differences were widespread at EPPO, and the lack of structured cultural integration led to divisions among employees. Spanish-speaking employees unintentionally excluded their Palestinian colleagues, leading to mistrust and social fragmentation. Without cultural audits or inclusive language policies, the workplace became segmented, undermining collaboration. Tensions increased and working relationships became strained, leading to inefficient project implementation.

Miguel’s leadership issues created a toxic work environment. His intrusive management style, including breaking into employees’ desks, helped to fuel resentment and mistrust. In addition, the lack of clear dispute resolution mechanisms meant that conflicts were either ignored or handled inappropriately.

Salary discrepancies led to dissatisfaction and disengagement among some employees. Moreover, the lack of performance-based resource allocation meant that financial decision making was inconsistent and ineffective, leading to wasted resources and a lack of accountability. These inconsistencies contributed to a general sense of frustration and mistrust among staff, which further undermined the stability of the project.

To mitigate these challenges, the European Commission can draw lessons from successful governance models in other regions. On issues of cultural integration, for example, inspiration can be drawn from the EIP in Singapore. The EIP is working to actively prevent cultural enclaves through structured diversity mandates. A similar approach in future EU projects, involving cultural audits and integration measures, could prevent divisions in the workplace and improve collaboration. In addition, establishing mentoring programmes between culturally diverse employees can help bridge divides and foster collaboration. El Salvador’s drastic reduction in gang violence through a hybrid model of security controls and social programmes demonstrates the importance of a conflict resolution framework. If the European Commission wanted to develop its own conflict resolution model, it should ideally include a range of mediation and conciliation protocols to resolve disputes early and constructively. In addition, special conflict resolution training should be offered to all managers. They should be equipped with the necessary tools to recognise, de-escalate and effectively resolve conflicts. Employees should also be consistently reminded to report any grievances they may experience in the workplace. Confidential channels should be established so that reports can be dealt with quickly and impartially.

For any project that wishes to avoid the same pitfalls that EPPO suffered from, facilitating conflict reconciliation is extremely important. The South African Reconciliation Commission paved the way for national healing by prioritising truth-telling and open dialogue. Similar reconciliation processes should be utilised, especially when a multinational project is located in an area where the wounds from past conflicts are still felt by the local population on a daily basis. Restorative justice should be an integral part of any conflict reconciliation measure. Two examples of widely successful restorative justice programmes from which potential projects could draw inspiration are the “conferences” of the Maori culture in New Zealand and the “circles” of the indigenous peoples of North America (Van Ness 2016). Furthermore, bias mitigation training should be provided to employees in the hopes of helping them recognise and overcome any unconscious biases that may hinder effective teamwork.

Finally, similar to South Korea’s post-war economic redevelopment, the project should introduce performance-based funding models that link the distribution of funds to clear, measurable results. In addition, the integration of dynamic workforce planning strategies, such as predictive analytics for staffing and resource allocation, can help ensure that staff are assigned based on skill requirements and project needs, thereby increasing overall efficiency.

None of this means, however, that the introduction of such practices is without potential challenges. The introduction of new governance frameworks can be slowed down by established bureaucratic processes and other entrenched practices, making it difficult to effectively implement the necessary reforms. This type of institutional inertia is common in such reform attempts. It is usually due to rigid hierarchies and an unwillingness to deviate from traditional methods. For example, it has been shown that a culture of excessive risk avoidance and deeply ingrained norms have greatly slowed down the introduction of new technologies in Japan (Chen 2008). Resistance from key decision makers who see change as a threat to their authority can also hinder progress. Overcoming these obstacles requires persistent lobbying, change management strategies and demonstrating the tangible benefits of proposed reforms through pilot programmes and gradual implementation.

Another potential challenge may arise from the adoption of certain conflict resolution strategies. Any introduction of conflict resolution strategies based on international models must be tailored to the specific political and cultural context of the countries in which they are to be implemented. Resistance may stem from deeply rooted social norms, historical grievances or power dynamics that influence decision-making processes. In addition, externally imposed frameworks may be perceived as foreign interventions, leading to reluctance or outright rejection by communities that prefer fully indigenous approaches to conflict resolution. To overcome these challenges, it is crucial to involve local stakeholders in the design and implementation of governance strategies and to ensure that any potential solutions are contextualised and in line with existing cultural frameworks. Additionally, promoting diplomatic dialogue and confidence-building initiatives can help bridge the gap between external policy makers and the local population, increasing the likelihood of successful adoption and implementation.

Finally, ensuring adequate financial and human resource investment in long-term governance improvements remains a challenge, especially when political cycles prioritise short-term results over sustainable institutional reforms (Ogami 2024). Budget constraints often lead to inconsistent funding, making it difficult to sustain governance improvements over multiple project cycles. In addition, competing political priorities can lead to resources being channelled into projects that promise immediate gains rather than those that require long-term investment. To overcome this obstacle, it is important to make multi-year funding commitments and ensure continuity of financial support across changing political governments. In addition, eliminating inefficiencies in financial management and reducing the misallocation of resources can enhance the impact of allocated funds.

To avoid the same shortcomings as the EPPO project and to increase the success of future projects, the European Commission should prioritise the adoption of intercultural integration strategies, clear conflict resolution mechanisms, structured reconciliation processes and optimal allocation of staff and resources. By incorporating these lessons into future project planning, the European Commission can create a framework that prevents conflict, promotes cooperation and ensures long-term project success. Through proactive measures, structured oversight and inclusive decision-making, EU-funded multinational projects can become models of efficiency, transparency and sustainability.
