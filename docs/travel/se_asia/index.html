Taiwan — Taipei

Taiwan is not a place most people plan to go. It tends to happen to you — a stopover that becomes a destination, a recommendation from someone who went for three days and stayed for three weeks, a country that quietly overdelivers on everything. Taipei is one of the most liveable cities in Asia: clean, fast, inexpensive, and dense with things to look at and eat.

The Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall is the kind of landmark that stops you mid-step. The plaza is enormous — white marble and open sky and the hall at the far end rising up with its blue octagonal roof. It is architecturally imposing but somehow still peaceful, especially in the early morning before the crowds arrive.

Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall — the scale of it doesn't translate in photos.

Longshan Temple is the opposite energy — old, incense-heavy, always busy with actual worshippers. Red lanterns, the smell of burning offerings, gold dragons wrapped around columns. It feels genuinely alive in a way that tourist temples often don’t.

Taipei 101 is worth doing once, mostly for the view from the top — the city spread out in every direction, mountains visible in the haze behind the suburban sprawl, and the sunset turning the whole skyline copper.

Longshan Temple.

Taipei 101 after dark.

The day trip to Jiufen is one of those experiences that earns its reputation. The old tea house street clings to the hillside above the sea, red lanterns strung across the narrow lanes, the harbour far below catching the last light. At night it looks like it was designed by a film set decorator — slightly too beautiful to be real.

Shifen, further along the same line, is where the sky lanterns go up. You write something on the paper, hold it while it fills with hot air, and let it go. Hundreds of them drift over the valley at any given time, catching the wind, slowly disappearing into the clouds above the gorge.

Shifen Old Street — sky lanterns drifting up over the valley.

Jiufen — the tea house lanterns after dark.

"A country that quietly overdelivers on everything."

Vietnam — Hanoi

Hanoi is louder, older, and considerably more chaotic than Taipei. The Old Quarter is a maze of narrow streets named after the trade they once specialised in — silk street, paper street, tin street — and crossing the road requires a specific kind of faith in the motorists. You just walk, steadily, and they flow around you.

The city is saturated with history in a way that is hard to avoid even if you wanted to. The Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum sits at the edge of Ba Dinh Square — austere and serious, the kind of place that quiets you down. The Temple of Literature nearby is the opposite: calm, green, beautiful in a classical Vietnamese way, willow trees around still ponds and stone steles listing the names of scholars from dynasties long gone.

Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum, Ba Dinh Square.

Temple of Literature — Hanoi's oldest university.

The street food in Hanoi is the best argument for going anywhere in Vietnam. Pho from a plastic stool at 7am, bun cha in the Old Quarter, banh mi from a cart on a corner. It is cheap, fast, and genuinely delicious in a way that makes you immediately recalibrate what you are willing to pay for food back home. The night market around Hoan Kiem Lake on weekends is the city at its most alive — families, tourists, street performers, and the lake itself lit up and calm in the middle of everything.

The Old Quarter after dark — every street corner is a restaurant.

Ha Long Bay

Ha Long Bay is one of those places where the photographs do not exaggerate. Nearly two thousand limestone karsts rising out of calm green water, mist sitting in the gaps between them in the early morning, fishing boats moving slowly through channels so narrow the stone walls rise on both sides. It is genuinely otherworldly.

The best way to see it is overnight on a junk boat — you sail out in the afternoon, anchor somewhere deep in the bay as the sun drops behind the karsts, and wake up to the mist before it burns off. Kayaking through the caves at low tide, the ceiling inches above your head, is the kind of thing that would be impossible to explain to someone who hasn’t done it.

Ha Long Bay — nearly two thousand karsts spread across 1,500 square kilometres.

Kayaking through the sea caves at low tide.

"You wake up to the mist before it burns off — limestone walls on every side."

On Vietnam & Taiwan

These are two countries that reward going in without too many expectations. Taiwan is efficient and gentle and full of surprises — the kind of place where you intended to spend a long weekend and find yourself genuinely sad to leave. Vietnam is rawer, louder, and more demanding, but it gives back more than it takes.

What they share is food, temples, and a density of things worth looking at that most European cities can’t match. If you are building a first trip to East Asia, this combination — a few days in Taipei, fly to Hanoi, day trip to Ha Long — is close to perfect.
