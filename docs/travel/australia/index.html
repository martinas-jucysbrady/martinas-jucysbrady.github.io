Busselton — Where It Started

There is something disorientating about landing in a country that looks vaguely familiar — same language, same culture — but feels undeniably foreign the moment you step outside (especially after a year in Japan). The light is different. The sky is bigger. The air smells like eucalyptus and salt.

Busselton was the first stop, a coastal town a couple of hours south of Perth that most people outside Australia have never heard of. It is the kind of place where the days move slowly and the sunsets take their time. The famous jetty stretches out over a kilometre into the Indian Ocean, long enough that by the end of it the shore behind you looks small and the horizon feels enormous.

The Busselton Jetty — the longest wooden jetty in the Southern Hemisphere.

The evenings in Busselton were what stuck with me most. The sky would shift from blue to pink to a deep purple without any urgency, the Norfolk pines along the foreshore silhouetted against it like something out of a film set. There were no crowds, no rush — just the low hum of a town winding down for the night.

Dusk along the Busselton foreshore — one of those skies you don’t quite believe is real.

“The sky would shift from blue to pink to a deep purple without any urgency.”

Perth — A City That Does Things Its Own Way {#perth} Perth is one of the most isolated cities on earth. The nearest comparable city is Adelaide, roughly 2,700 kilometres away. And yet it doesn’t feel cut off — if anything, it feels free. Like it never had to answer to anyone, so it just built itself the way it wanted to.

The city sits along the Swan River, with the Indian Ocean beaches within easy reach to the west. Cottesloe, the most famous of them, is everything a beach should be: wide, clean, with turquoise water and the kind of afternoon light that makes everything look golden. The café strips along the city and suburbs are excellent — Perth has quietly become one of the better food cities in Australia, a fact it seems in no rush to advertise.

Kings Park sits on a bluff above the city, 400 hectares of bush and botanic garden with a view across the river to the skyline. It is the kind of place that reminds you how recently cities like this were carved out of wilderness — you can stand in native bushland and look out at glass skyscrapers at the same time. That contrast is very Perth.

Perth from above — Kings Park looking out over the Swan River.

Sydney — The One That Needs No Introduction {#sydney} Flying east across the continent takes about five hours, crossing deserts and red earth that feel genuinely ancient. Sydney announces itself before you land — the harbour glittering from the air, the Opera House unmistakable even from altitude.

It is a city that knows it is beautiful and doesn’t apologise for it. The harbour is everywhere, cutting through the middle of everything, and the best way to understand the city’s geography is to get on a ferry. The Manly Ferry is the classic — thirty minutes across the harbour from Circular Quay, watching the Bridge get smaller behind you as the Pacific opens up ahead.

Sydney Harbour Bridge from the Manly Ferry — one of those views that earns its reputation.

Bondi is Bondi. It is busy and sun-drenched and full of people who seem to have figured out something about how to live. The coastal walk from Bondi to Coogee is one of the best urban walks in the world — clifftops, sea pools, and ocean as far as you can see. Do it in the morning before it gets crowded.

The Blue Mountains sit a couple of hours west of the city and feel like a completely different world. The Three Sisters rock formation rising out of the valley below Echo Point is one of those landscapes that looks like a screensaver until you’re standing in front of it and realise it’s genuinely, absurdly beautiful.

On Australia Three weeks is not enough. Australia is the kind of place where you spend the whole trip recalibrating your sense of scale — of distance, of sky, of time. The distances between places feel genuinely vast, the nature genuinely wild, and the people genuinely relaxed about all of it.

What struck me most, travelling between two completely different coasts of the same country, was how un-monolithic it is. Busselton and Sydney might as well be different countries. The west is quiet and wide open; the east is confident and turned outward toward the world. Both feel distinctly Australian in ways that are hard to explain until you’ve been to both.

I will go back. There is too much left: the Red Centre, Queensland, Tasmania, the Kimberley. Australia rewards return visits, and I already know I owe it more than three weeks.

Somewhere between Busselton and everything else.
